# Philosophy

The problem isn't that the AI isn't smart enough. That's the easy complaint, and it's mostly wrong. Claude is capable. It reasons well, catches mistakes, asks good questions when pointed in the right direction. The problem is simpler and more structural: every session starts cold.

Every correction evaporates. You tell the AI not to suggest that library. It doesn't — until you open a new session. Every piece of context you rebuilt by hand on Monday is context you'll rebuild again next Monday. The model hasn't forgotten anything. It never had access. Statelessness isn't a bug in the AI; it's a gap in the infrastructure around the AI. And gaps in infrastructure are fixable.

We built this because most systems only address half the problem. They capture explicit knowledge: rules, principles, what to do in general. The CLAUDE.md carries this. It's the operating manual — behavioral instructions that load automatically at the start of every session. Most advanced Claude Code users have some version of this. It's necessary. It isn't sufficient.

What's missing is a place for experiential knowledge. The stuff that's only true for this project, this week. The custom integration in lib/stripe/. The open issue with the webhook retry logic. The correction you made last Friday that you want to still be true on Monday. The playbook carries this — active projects, session logs, a feedback log. The AI reads it before touching anything. You both maintain it. The combination of the two files is the system. One handles principles; the other handles memory. That distinction is the insight.

The feedback loop is the core mechanic. Corrections that don't get written down evaporate. A rule born from three sessions of the same frustration is worth more than a principle someone thought up in advance. The feedback log isn't a feature — it's the whole point. It starts thin. It gets specific. By month two it's doing work you can feel. A vague entry like "AI was bad at CSS" is noise. The entry that actually changes behavior is: "Don't use inline styles for layout — always flexbox or grid, because inline styles break at the first media query." The specificity is the mechanism.

Templates resist use until they don't. The spec templates feel like overhead right up until they've saved you 30 minutes of rework. The psychology is real: the payoff isn't immediate, and most people don't invest in tools that don't pay off in the first session. The answer isn't simpler templates — it's building the habit that no work starts without a spec, even a micro one. A micro-spec takes 90 seconds and prevents an hour of misaligned building. Once the habit is there, the templates become fast. The resistance was never about the templates.

The compounding return is real, but it doesn't arrive in session one. Session 1 is modest. Session 10 is different. Session 30 is different in kind. The AI that opens session 30 knows the project's idioms, has absorbed 20+ of your corrections, knows which assumptions to never make. That's not magic. It's maintenance. The system is the habit that makes the maintenance happen.

Fork it. The version of this system you have in 3 months should look different from what you installed. Your rules should reflect your frustrations, not ours. Your feedback log should be full of entries we've never seen. Your playbook should have projects we don't know about. The templates will drift. The correction patterns will be yours. When this template has drifted beyond recognition into something that fits you exactly — that's success. That's the loop closing.
